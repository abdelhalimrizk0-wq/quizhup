Diabetic Emergencies Quiz

تم صنع هذا الامتحان بواسطة عبدالحليم رزق

1. According to the American Diabetes Association (ADA) definition provided, severe hypoglycemia is defined as:

Rationale: The text states, "The American Diabetes Association (ADA) now defines... severe hypoglycemia is defined as any low blood glucose value associated with cognitive impairment."

2. Which of the following oral hypoglycemic agents is specifically mentioned as being associated with hypoglycemia in type 2 diabetics?

Rationale: The document says, "Hypoglycemia also can occur with type 2 diabetics who are treated with oral hypoglycemic agents, especially the sulfonylureas: glipizide, glyburide, and glimepiride..."

3. What is the first-line defense mechanism against hypoglycemia?

Rationale: The text identifies the "first defense against hypoglycemia is pancreatic beta-cell (insulin secretion decreases) which increases glycogenolysis and gluconeogenesis."

4. Hypoglycemic unawareness, associated with HAAF, is defined as:

Rationale: The text defines it: "Hypoglycemic unawareness is defined as the loss of adrenergic symptoms of hypoglycemia that prompt a patient to act to prevent the progression of severe hypoglycemia..."

5. All of the following are listed as causes of *fasting* hypoglycemia EXCEPT:

Rationale: The text lists "Fasting hypoglycemia: excessive insulin dosage, Insulinomas... heart failure." It lists "Postprandial hypoglycemia: ... gastric surgery". Therefore, gastric surgery is a cause of postprandial, not fasting, hypoglycemia.

6. How does alcohol intake primarily cause hypoglycemia?

Rationale: The text states, "Alcohol shuts off gluconeogenesis by interfering with intermediate biochemical steps in the liver."

7. Which of the following is classified as a *neuroglycopenic* (CNS) symptom of hypoglycemia, as opposed to an adrenergic one?

Rationale: "Moderate to severe hypoglycemic symptoms (CNS or neuroglycopenic symptoms): Headache... Confusion, Impaired judgment, Slurred speech...". The other options are listed under "Mild hypoglycemic symptoms (adrenergic response)".

8. What is the "Rule of 15" in hypoglycemia therapy?

Rationale: The document advises to "Consider the 'rule of 15' during therapy (i.e., 15 g of carbohydrate will raise the glucose level about 15 mg/dl in about 15 minutes)."

9. According to the management protocol, what is the correct IV bolus for a patient with *severe* hypoglycemia (blood glucose < 40 mg/dL)?

Rationale: For a "Blood glucose level lower than 40 mg/dL (severe hypoglycemia): Administer 50 mL dextrose (50%) in water (D50W) as an IV bolus."

10. True or False: Cortisol and growth hormone are released as the *first* defense against hypoglycemia.

Rationale: False. The text states the first defense is decreased insulin secretion and the second is increased glucagon. "When hypoglycemia is prolonged beyond 4 hours, cortisol and growth hormone also support glucose production...".

11. True or False: Diaphoresis (sweating) is listed as the most common symptom of the mild hypoglycemic (adrenergic) response.

Rationale: True. The text lists under "Mild hypoglycemic symptoms (adrenergic response): Diaphoresis (most common)...".

12. True or False: Postprandial hypoglycemia typically occurs more than 5 hours after a meal.

Rationale: False. The text defines "fasting hypoglycemia (> 5 hours after a meal) and postprandial hypoglycemia (1-2 hours after a meal)."

13. Which of the following is *not* one of the "five Is" listed as causes of DKA?

Rationale: The five "Is" listed are Infection, Inadequate insulin, Initial presentation, Intensive Clinical stressors, and Medication-induced impairment. "Ingestion (Alcohol)" is not on this list (though alcohol is listed as a cause of *hypo*glycemia).

14. What are the three major physiological disturbances in DKA?

Rationale: The document states, "Three major physiological disturbances exist in DKA: (1) hyperosmolality from hyperglycemia, (2) metabolic acidosis from accumulation of ketoacids, and (3) volume depletion from osmotic diuresis."

15. At what blood glucose threshold does glucose begin to escape into the urine (glucosuria) because the reabsorption capacity of the tubules is exceeded?

Rationale: "However, when the blood glucose level exceeds the normal threshold of about 180 mg/dL, glucose begins to escape into the urine because the reabsorption capacity of the tubules is exceeded."

16. The characteristic "fruity odor" of the breath in DKA is caused by the excretion of which substance in the lungs?

Rationale: "Acetone is safely excreted in the lungs, causing the characteristic fruity odor." and "Acetone is exhaled, giving the breath its characteristic fruity odor.".

17. What is the correct formula provided for calculating the anion gap?

Rationale: "The following formula is used to calculate the anion gap: (Sodium) - (chloride +HCO3) The normal value is less than 15 mEq/L".

18. All of the following are clinical manifestations of DKA EXCEPT:

Rationale: The text lists "polyuria... polydipsia...", "sunken eyeballs", "Tachycardia and hypotension", and "Kussmaul respirations". Therefore, bradycardia and hypertension are incorrect; the opposite is true.

19. Euglycemic diabetic ketoacidosis (EDKA) is primarily related to:

Rationale: "EDKA is primarily related to an imbalance between insulin and counter-regulatory hormones, with an elevated glucagon/insulin ratio leading to increased lipolysis."

20. A key difference in the presentation of EDKA compared to hyperglycemic DKA is that EDKA patients may *not* have:

Rationale: The text notes EDKA presentation is "similar... to hyperglycemic DKA, although perhaps without polyuria, polydipsia..." and "Patients may not have polydipsia or polyuria since serum glucose is normal.".

21. What is the initial recommended fluid for reversing dehydration in a severely dehydrated DKA patient?

Rationale: "Intravenous isotonic normal saline (0.9% NaCl) is infused to replenish the vascular deficit..." and "For the severely dehydrated patient, 1 L of normal saline is infused immediately.".

22. According to the DKA management protocol, IV sodium bicarbonate is *not* recommended for patients with a pH above what level?

Rationale: "Use of IV sodium bicarbonate is not recommended for patients with a pH > 7.0 in DKA.". The protocol confirms this by stating to give bicarb only "If pH < 6.9".

23. When managing DKA, why are IV insulin infusions preferred over subcutaneous injections?

Rationale: "IV insulin infusions are preferred over subcutaneous insulin injections due to insufficient tissue absorption in the presence of hypotension, generalized edema, and use of vasopressors."

24. What is the target blood glucose range to maintain in a DKA patient *after* the initial reduction (when the infusion is reduced)?

Rationale: The protocol states: "Keep serum glucose between 150 and 200 mg/dL until resolution of DKA."

25. True or False: DKA is defined as acute hyperglycemia *without* acidosis.

Rationale: False. "DKA is defined as acute hyperglycemia with acidosis (ketotic)...".

26. True or False: The diagnostic criteria for DKA include a blood glucose greater than 250 mg/dL and a serum bicarbonate less than 18 mEq/L.

Rationale: True. The criteria listed are: "Blood glucose greater than 250 mg/dL", "pH less than 7.3", and "Serum bicarbonate less than 18 mEq/L".

27. True or False: In DKA, Kussmaul respirations are deep, rapid breaths that attempt to release carbonic acid as carbon dioxide.

Rationale: True. "Breathing becomes deep and rapid (Kussmaul respirations) to release carbonic acid in the form of carbon dioxide."

28. True or False: When transitioning from IV to SC insulin, the IV infusion should be stopped *immediately* before the first SC dose is given.

Rationale: False. "To transfer from IV to SC, continue IV insulin infusion for 1-2 hr after SC insulin is begun to ensure adequate plasma insulin levels."

29. What is the primary reason that ketosis is absent or mild in Hyperosmolar Hyperglycemic State (HHS)?

Rationale: In HHS, "because there is some insulin secretion, lipolysis (fat breakdown) is suppressed. (Mild or absent ketone formation)".

30. All of the following are diagnostic criteria for HHS EXCEPT:

Rationale: The criteria for HHS include "Absent or mild ketonuria". Moderate or severe ketonuria is a diagnostic criterion for DKA.

31. What is listed as the *primary reason* that type 2 diabetics develop HHS?

Rationale: "Infection is the primary reason that type 2 diabetics develop HHS; the most common infections are pneumonia and urinary tract infections."

32. Which of these symptoms is characteristic of DKA but *absent* in HHS?

Rationale: The text states that in HHS, "there is no overproduction of ketones and no specific physical signs and symptoms of ketosis (no Kussmaul respirations...)"

33. What is the *primary intervention* for HHS?

Rationale: "The primary intervention for HHS is rapid rehydration to restore the intravascular volume."

34. When managing HHS, at what blood glucose level should the IV fluids be changed to 5% dextrose with 0.45% NaCl?

Rationale: The protocol states: "When serum glucose reaches 200 mg/dL (DKA) or 300 mg/dL (HHS): Change to 5% dextrose with 0.45% NaCl...".

35. True or False: HHS typically has a rapid, obvious onset over a few hours.

Rationale: False. "HHS has a slow, subtle onset and develops over several days.".

36. True or False: Mortality rates are documented as being higher with HHS than with DKA.

Rationale: True. "Mortality rates are higher with HHS, because of the severe volume loss and because it occurs more frequently in older adults.".

37. According to the management protocol, if a patient's serum potassium (K+) is < 3.3 mEq/L, what is the *immediate* priority?

Rationale: The protocol is explicit: "If K+ < 3.3 mEq/L: Hold insulin and give 20-30 mEq/hr until K+ is > 3.3 mEq/L.". Such a low potassium level is a contraindication for starting insulin, which would drive potassium even lower, causing dangerous arrhythmias.

38. According to the management protocol, if a patient's serum glucose does not fall by at least 10% in the first hour of an insulin infusion, what is the next step?

Rationale: "If serum glucose does not fall by at least 10% in the first hour: Give 0.14 U/kg as IV bolus, then continue previous Rx."

39. True or False: A normal fasting blood glucose level in a healthy person ranges between 100 and 120 mg/dL.

Rationale: False. "Normal fasting blood glucose levels range between 70 and 100 mg/dL in a healthy person.".

40. True or False: In the management protocol for DKA/HHS, if a patient's corrected serum Na+ is high, the recommended IV fluid is 0.45% NaCl.

Rationale: True. The protocol states: "Evaluate corrected serum Na+: Serum Na+ high: 0.45% NaCl ( 250-500 mL/hr)...".
