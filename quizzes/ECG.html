1. Where is the electrical stimulus for a normal heartbeat generated?

Rationale: "An electrical stimulus is generated by the sinus node (also called the sinoatrial node, or SA node). This is a small mass of specialized tissue located in the right upper chamber (atria) of the heart."

2. What is the normal rate at which the SA node generates an electrical stimulus?

Rationale: "The sinus node generates an electrical stimulus regularly, 60 to 100 times per minute under normal conditions."

3. Where in the conduction pathway are electrical impulses slowed down for a very short period?

Rationale: "The electrical impulse travels from the sinus node to the atrioventricular node (also called AV node). There, impulses are slowed down for a very short period..."

4. The Bundle of His divides into which two pathways?

Rationale: "The bundle of His divides into right and left pathways, called bundle branches, to stimulate the right and left ventricles."

5. Which waveform on an ECG represents the electrical stimulation of the atria?

Rationale: "Electrical stimulation of the atria produces the P wave in an ECG and results in coordinated mechanical contraction (atrial systole)."

6. The QRS complex on an ECG represents what?

Rationale: "Electrical stimulation of the ventricles produces the QRS complex on an ECG and results in coordinated ventricular contraction (ventricular systole)."

7. What does the T wave in an ECG represent?

Rationale: "The T wave in an ECG is the result of the relaxation of cardiac muscle." This is also known as ventricular repolarization.

8. All of the following are purposes of an ECG EXCEPT:

Rationale: An ECG measures electrical activity. It can *imply* chamber size, but it does not *directly measure* blood pressure.

9. Which of the following is an indication for an ECG?

Rationale: The document lists chest/epigastric pain, abnormal pulse, and syncope as indications for an ECG.

10. True or False: An ECG is sometimes performed prior to general anesthetic medications.

Rationale: True. The document lists "Sometimes prior to general anesthetic medications" as an indication.

11. What is the recommended patient position for a standard 12-lead ECG?

Rationale: "Ensure the patient is lying down in a comfortable position ideally resting against a pillow at an angle of 45° with the head well supported."

12. Which limb lead is placed on the inner right wrist?

Rationale: "Red to the inner right wrist."

13. Which limb lead is placed on the inner left leg, just above the ankle?

Rationale: "Green to the inner left leg, just above the ankle."

14. Where is the V1 (white/red lead) chest electrode placed?

Rationale: "V1 (white/red lead): fourth intercostal space, just to the right of the sternum."

15. Where is the V2 (white/yellow lead) chest electrode placed?

Rationale: "V2 (white/yellow lead): fourth intercostal space, just to the left of the sternum."

16. The V4 (white/brown lead) electrode is placed at which landmark?

Rationale: "V4 (white/brown lead): fifth intercostal space, mid-clavicular line."

17. Where is the V3 (white/green lead) electrode placed?

Rationale: "V3 (white/green lead): midway between V2 and V4."

18. The V6 (white/violet lead) is placed on the mid-axillary line, on the same horizontal line as which other leads?

Rationale: "V6 (white/violet lead): mid-axillary line, on the same horizontal line as V4 and V5."

19. What is the standard paper speed for an ECG recording?

Rationale: "The paper speed is usually 25 mm/s."

20. On standard ECG paper, one small square (1 mm) horizontally represents how much time?

Rationale: "Horizontally, one small square (1mm) = 0.04 seconds."

21. One large square (5 mm) horizontally represents how much time?

Rationale: "Horizontally, one large square (5 mm) = 0.20 seconds."

22. What is the standard calibration for voltage (vertically)?

Rationale: "Standard calibration is 10 mm/mV." and "Vertically, one large square (5mm) = 0.5 mV." Both mean the same.

23. In which leads are P waves normally upright?

Rationale: "P waves are usually upright in leads facing the inferior surface of the heart (leads II, III and aVF) and inverted in aVR..."

24. What is the normal duration (width) of a PR interval?

Rationale: "The normal PR interval is 0.12-0.20 s (3-5 small squares)."

25. If a patient has a regular rhythm and there are 4 large squares between QRS complexes, what is the heart rate?

Rationale: "Estimate the QRS rate by counting the number of large squares between two adjacent QRS complexes and dividing it into 300." (300 / 4 = 75).

26. A heart rate slower than 60/min is called:

Rationale: "Slow QRS rate (bradycardia): <60/min."

27. What is the normal width for a QRS complex?

Rationale: The normal width for a QRS complex is "< 0.12 s (3 small squares)."

28. The ST segment is normally:

Rationale: "Examine the ST segment... It is usually isoelectric." (Meaning it rests on the baseline).

29. What does the QT interval represent?

Rationale: "It represents the total duration of ventricular depolarization and repolarization. It is measured from the beginning of the QRS complex to the end of the T wave."

30. What is the normal range for the electrical axis?

Rationale: "Normal electrical axis ranges between -30° to +90°."
