1. What is the most common route for emergency drug administration in critically ill patients due to its rapid effect?

Rationale: "Intravenous: and it is the most common method of administration in critically ill patients because of its rapid effect."

2. The acronym NAVEL is used to remember drugs that can be given via which route?

Rationale: NAVEL (Narcan, Atropine, Valium, Epinephrine, Lidocaine) are examples of drugs that can be administered "Through endotracheal tube (ETT)."

3. When giving a drug via ETT, the dose is typically how many times that of an IV dose?

Rationale: "The dose is 2 to 2.5 times that of an IV dose and is followed by 10 cm NS."

4. Which of the following drugs is listed as one that should NOT be diluted, especially during CPR?

Rationale: Under "Drugs not to be diluted," the text lists "Sodium bicarbonate (especially in CPR)."

5. Which drug is listed as one that "may be diluted only in 10 ml NS"?

Rationale: The document states "Morphine diluted in NS" under the category "Drugs may be diluted only in 10 ml NS then given."

6. MONA therapy is used in Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS). What does the 'M' stand for?

Rationale: "MONA therapy: M (morphine), O (oxygen), N (nitroglycerin), A (aspirin) are used in acute coronary syndrome (ACS)."

7. When administering oxygen to a patient with COPD, what is a key nursing consideration?

Rationale: A nursing consideration for Oxygen is to "Avoid high flow administration in patients with concurrent COPD."

8. What is the primary action of Aspirin when used to treat ACS?

Rationale: The action of Aspirin in ACS is that it "Slows platelet aggregation, reducing the risk of further occlusion or reocclusion..."

9. What is the standard recommended Aspirin dosage to treat ACS?

Rationale: "The standard recommended aspirin dosage to treat ACS is 160 to 325 mg." Patients are often instructed to chew it to speed absorption.

10. Nitroglycerin is classified as an anti-angina, anti-hypertension, and a:

Rationale: Nitroglycerin is classified as an "Anti-angina - vasodilator - anti-hypertension."

11. What effect does Nitroglycerin have on myocardial oxygen consumption?

Rationale: "Decreases myocardial oxygen consumption, allowing the heart to work with a lower oxygen demand."

12. Which drug is the analgesic of choice for pain in myocardial infarction and also helps relieve pulmonary congestion?

Rationale: "Morphine... Analgesic of choice for pain in myocardial infarction... Relieves pulmonary congestion, lowers myocardial oxygen requirement and reduces anxiety."

13. What is a critical nursing consideration before administering Morphine?

Rationale: "Obtain baseline respiratory rate, depth, and rhythm and size of pupils before administering the drug" due to its potential for respiratory depression.

14. Amiodarone (Cordarone) is classified as what class of anti-dysrhythmic?

Rationale: The document lists Amiodarone as a "Class III Antiarrhythmic."

15. What is the FIRST choice drug for treating symptomatic bradycardia?

Rationale: Atropine is indicated for "Symptomatic bradycardia (first choice)."

16. What is the standard IV push dose for Atropine?

Rationale: The dosage for Atropine is "0.5mg IV push and may repeat up to a total dose of 2mg."

17. The action of Dopamine is described as:

Rationale: Dopamine's action is "dose-related," stimulating different receptors (dopaminergic, beta1, alpha) at different infusion rates.

18. What is the effect of a low dose (<5 mcg/kg/min) of Dopamine?

Rationale: "At low rates of infusion (<5 mcg/kg/min) (renal dose): Affects dopaminergic receptors, causing vasodilation in the renal, mesenteric, coronary, and intracerebral vascular beds... thus increasing urine output."

19. At an intermediate dose (5-10 mcg/kg/min), Dopamine primarily affects which receptors?

Rationale: "At intermediate rates of infusion (5-10 mcg/kg/min) (cardiac dose): Affects the beta1-adrenoceptors, resulting in improved myocardial contractility..."

20. What is the effect of a high dose (>10 mcg/kg/min) of Dopamine?

Rationale: "At higher rates of infusion (>10 mcg/kg/min) (pressor): Affects alpha-adrenoceptors, with consequent vasoconstrictor effects and a rise in blood pressure."

21. Which drug is an inotrope of choice for heart failure, as it increases contractility without a significant change in blood pressure?

Rationale: Dobutamine "Increases myocardial contractility & cardiac output without significant change in blood pressure."

22. What must be corrected before starting a Dobutamine or Dopamine infusion?

Rationale: For both Dopamine ("Correct... hypovolemia") and Dobutamine ("Fluid deficit (Hypovolemia) should be corrected before infusion"), adequate fluid volume is required.

23. What is the standard dose of Epinephrine for a patient in cardiac arrest?

Rationale: "For patients in cardiac arrest: give 1 mg intravenously... every 3 to 5 minutes."

24. Which drug is a vasopressor indicated for hypotension and shock that increases blood flow to vital organs without increasing the workload of the heart?

Rationale: Noradrenaline (Nor-epinephrine, Levophed) "increases blood flow to all vital organs without increasing the workload or output of the heart."

25. Calcium Chloride is an indicated treatment for all of the following EXCEPT:

Rationale: Calcium Chloride is indicated for hyperkalemia, hypocalcemia, and calcium channel overdose. Atropine is the first-line drug for symptomatic bradycardia.

26. True or False: Calcium Chloride and Sodium Bicarbonate can be administered through the same IV line without flushing.

Rationale: False. "IV line should be flushed between calcium chloride and sodium bicarbonate administration." They will precipitate (form a solid) if mixed.

27. Which emergency drug is indicated for Torsade de pointes?

Rationale: Magnesium Sulfate is indicated for "torsade de pointes."

28. What is the antidote for Magnesium Sulfate toxicity (e.g., respiratory depression or heart block)?

Rationale: A nursing consideration for Magnesium Sulfate is: "*Calcium 5 to 10 meq can be given to reverse respiratory depression and heart block."

29. Sodium Bicarbonate is indicated for the correction of:

Rationale: Sodium Bicarbonate is an "electrolyte replenisher and systemic alkalizer" indicated for the "Correction of severe metabolic acidosis..."

30. What is a key nursing consideration when administering Sodium Bicarbonate?

Rationale: Nursing considerations include: "Obtain arterial blood PH, PO2, PaCO2 serum calcium, sodium, potassium balance and renal function before and throughout the therapy."
