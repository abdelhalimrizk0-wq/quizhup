1. How is Status Epilepticus primarily defined regarding duration?

Rationale: Status epilepticus is defined as a condition of either continued seizure activity or repetitive seizures without interictal recovery over a period exceeding 30 minutes.

2. Which areas of the brain are identified as the most common sites of seizure origin?

Rationale: The text states that the most common sites of seizure origin are the frontal and temporal lobes, particularly the hippocampus in the medial temporal lobe.

3. A patient presents with "side-to-side head movements" and "purposeful activity" during an episode. There is no postictal phase. This clinical presentation is most consistent with:

Rationale: Psychogenic nonepileptic seizures are differentiated from epilepsy by asymmetrical motor activity, side-to-side head movements, purposeful activity, gradual onset, and the lack of a "postictal" phase.

4. During a generalized seizure, the brain's metabolic demand for oxygen increases. If this demand is not met, what is the direct consequence?

Rationale: The brain's metabolic demand for oxygen increases dramatically during a seizure. If this demand isn't met, hypoxia and brain damage result.

5. A nurse is caring for a patient experiencing status epilepticus. What are the priority goals of management?

Rationale: The stated goals for management are: maintain airway, breathing, circulation; stop seizures; stabilize patient; and identify/treat causes.

6. Which class of medications is typically administered FIRST as an emergency treatment for status epilepticus to stop the seizure activity?

Rationale: Administration of diazepam (Valium) or lorazepam (Ativan) is the emergency treatment to halt seizures. They are short-acting drugs used as a starting dose.

7. While administering Benzodiazepines, the nurse understands that simultaneous loading with which medication is necessary because Benzodiazepines are short-acting?

Rationale: Because Benzodiazepines are short-acting, simultaneous loading with phenytoin or fosphenytoin is necessary for longer-term control.

8. Which of the following describes "Refractory status epilepticus"?

Rationale: Refractory status epilepticus refers to continuing seizures (convulsive or non-convulsive) despite appropriate antiepileptic drugs. It often requires continuous infusion treatment.

9. A nurse witnesses a patient having a tonic-clonic seizure. What is the most appropriate INITIAL nursing action?

Rationale: The nurse should ease the patient to the floor and protect the head with a pad to prevent injury. Insertion of objects or restraints can cause injury (broken teeth, fractures) and is contraindicated.

10. Why is Thiamine I.V. specifically indicated in the management of status epilepticus for certain patients?

Rationale: Thiamine I.V. is indicated specifically in cases of chronic alcoholism or withdrawal as part of the management protocol.

11. Which diagnostic test is used to confirm the diagnosis of epilepsy by providing evidence of the continuing tendency to have seizures?

Rationale: The EEG is used to confirm the diagnosis of epilepsy by providing evidence of the continuing tendency to have seizures (detecting electrical activity).

12. What distinguishes a "Partial Seizure" from a "Generalized Seizure"?

Rationale: The text explains that the impulse cascades to one side of the brain in a partial seizure, whereas it cascades to both sides of the brain in a generalized seizure.

13. Which type of seizure is characterized by tonic-clonic convulsions associated with increased body temperature and is more common in children?

Rationale: Febrile epileptic seizures are defined as tonic-clonic convulsions associated with increased body temperature and are more common in children.

14. Following the "ictal" period of a seizure, the nurse assesses the patient in the "postictal" phase. What findings are expected?

Rationale: The actual period of the seizure (ictal period) may be followed by a postictal phase of lethargy and disorientation.

15. Primary causes of status epilepticus are often idiopathic and occur most often in which age group?

Rationale: Primary causes are idiopathic without apparent structural changes and occur most often in children younger than 10 years.

16. The nurse finds a patient with clenched jaws during a seizure. What should the nurse AVOID doing?

Rationale: Do not attempt to pry open jaws that are clenched in a spasm or to insert anything. Broken teeth and injury to the lips/tongue may result.

17. When administering antiseizure medications and sedatives to halt seizures, what is the nurse's specific monitoring priority due to side effects?

Rationale: The nurse initiates ongoing assessment of respiratory and cardiac function because of the risk for delayed depression of respiration and blood pressure secondary to these medications.

18. During a seizure, how should the patient be positioned if possible, and why?

Rationale: The patient is turned to a side-lying position (with head flexed forward) to assist in draining pharyngeal secretions (saliva/mucus) and prevent aspiration.

19. Vagus nerve stimulation is a nonpharmacologic approach that acts similarly to:

Rationale: The vagus nerve stimulation device acts on the brain the way a pacemaker acts on the heart, sending electrical signals to inhibit seizure activity.

20. Patients on long-term antiseizure therapy are at significant risk for which specific side effect, requiring injury protection?

Rationale: A person who has received long-term antiseizure therapy has a significant risk for fractures as a side effect of therapy.

21. Which type of generalized seizure is described as "brief staring, usually without motor involvement" and is also known as petit mal?

Rationale: Absence (petit mal) seizures are characterized by brief staring, usually without motor involvement.

22. If an "aura" precedes the seizure, what specific nursing intervention may be possible to reduce injury?

Rationale: If an aura precedes the seizure, the nurse may insert an oral airway to reduce the possibility of the patient's biting the tongue or cheek. (Note: This is only if it precedes the seizure; never force during a seizure).

23. In the management of status epilepticus, why might 50% dextrose I.V. be administered?

Rationale: 50% dextrose I.V. is indicated when seizures are secondary to hypoglycemia (low blood sugar), which can be a cause of seizures.

24. Which of the following are characteristic features of Generalized Convulsive Status Epilepticus?

Rationale: Generalized convulsive SE involves both hemispheres and is characterized by complete loss of consciousness, incontinence, and tongue biting.

25. What is the key difference between Simple Partial and Complex Partial seizures regarding consciousness?

Rationale: Simple partial seizures (like Jacksonian) present with no change in LOC, while Complex partial seizures involve altered LOC.

26. Epilepsy is defined as a condition where seizures are:

Rationale: The text defines epilepsy as a condition in which seizures are spontaneous and recurrent.

27. Which ions are regulated by receptor channels and determine the electrical activity of the neuronal membrane?

Rationale: The electrical activity of the neuronal membrane is determined by the flow of Na+, K+, Ca2+, and Cl- between intracellular and extracellular spaces.

28. A "Jacksonian" seizure is an example of which type of seizure?

Rationale: The text categorizes Jacksonian seizures under Simple Partial seizures (specifically involving the frontal lobe motor area).

29. If a patient becomes agitated in the postictal phase, what is the best nursing approach?

Rationale: If the patient becomes agitated after a seizure (postictal), the nurse should use calm persuasion and gentle restraint to ensure safety without escalating agitation.

30. What specialized diet is appropriate for children with intractable seizures?

Rationale: The text mentions knowing the specifics of a ketogenic (high-fat, low-carbohydrate) diet, when appropriate, for children with intractable seizures.
