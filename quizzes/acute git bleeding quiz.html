Acute GI Bleeding Quiz

تم صنع هذا الامتحان بواسطة عبدالحليم رزق

1. A patient with hepatic cirrhosis presents with hematemesis. What is the most likely *underlying* cause of the bleeding?

Rationale: Esophagogastric varices are engorged blood vessels that develop as a result of portal hypertension, which is secondary to hepatic cirrhosis[cite: 62]. While the other options are causes of UGI bleeding, varices are directly linked to the pathophysiology of cirrhosis.

2. Why are hemoglobin and hematocrit considered poor indicators of *acute* blood loss severity?

Rationale: Hemoglobin and hematocrit are poor indicators if the bleeding is acute[cite: 109]. The initial values may be normal because the loss of plasma and RBCs is equal. It can take 4 to 6 hours after fluid replacement (or up to 72 hours) for the values to reflect the actual blood loss[cite: 109, 110].

3. True or False: Hematochezia (bright red stools) definitively indicates a lower GI bleed.

Rationale: False. While hematochezia most often signifies lower GI bleeding [cite: 34], it can also be due to a brisk upper GI bleed with a rapid transit time through the bowel in 10 to 15% of patients[cite: 35].

4. A patient presents with acute hematemesis, a BP of 85/50 mmHg, and a heart rate of 125 bpm. What is the *first* management priority?

Rationale: The patient is showing signs of hypovolemic shock (hypotension, tachycardia)[cite: 94, 96]. The initial management priority is resuscitation. This includes establishing IV access with at least two large-bore peripheral IVs (18 gauge or larger) and starting fluid replacement therapy with an isotonic crystalloid solution[cite: 124, 125].

5. What is the anatomic landmark that differentiates upper GI bleeding from lower GI bleeding?

Rationale: The ligament of Treitz is the anatomic division used to differentiate between the two areas[cite: 25]. Bleeding proximal (above) this ligament is upper GI, and bleeding distal (below) it is lower GI[cite: 26, 27].

6. A patient describes their emesis as looking like "coffee grounds." This sign (hematemesis) indicates that the blood has...

Rationale: Hematemesis appearing as brown "coffee grounds" means that the blood has been in the stomach for some time [cite: 33] and has been altered by gastric HCl acid. In contrast, bright red arterial blood has *not* been in contact with gastric acid[cite: 30].

7. Which patient is at the *highest* risk for developing Stress-Related Erosive Syndrome (SRES)?

Rationale: SRES, or stress ulcers, are abnormalities found in critically ill patients[cite: 49]. All critically ill patients are at risk [cite: 52], but those at greatest risk include patients in high physiologic stress situations, such as mechanical ventilation[cite: 55, 56].

8. What is the primary therapeutic goal of gastric fluid pH monitoring for a patient at risk for SRES?

Rationale: For patients at risk for SRES, routine assessments should include gastric fluid pH monitoring with a goal of keeping the pH greater than 4[cite: 54]. This is often achieved with IV proton pump inhibitors[cite: 132].

9. True or False: Melena (black, tarry stools) is indicative of upper GI bleeding in 90% of cases.

Rationale: True. Melena, which is a foul-smelling, black, tarry stool, occurs with slow bleeding from an upper GI source [cite: 36] and is indicative of UGI bleeding in 90% of cases[cite: 37].

10. A Mallory-Weiss tear, a cause of UGI bleeding, is most often associated with...

Rationale: Mallory-Weiss tears are lacerations of the distal esophagus at the gastroesophageal junction[cite: 64]. They are often associated with heavy alcohol use and a history of forceful vomiting, retching, or violent coughing[cite: 64].

11. What is the *leading* cause of upper GI hemorrhage?

Rationale: While peptic ulcer disease is the most common cause of UGI bleeding [cite: 43], the *leading* cause contributing to these ulcers is the bacterial action of *H. pylori*, which is responsible for 50-70% of cases[cite: 45].

12. A patient's stool is black and tarry. Why does this (melena) occur?

Rationale: Melena occurs with slow bleeding from an upper GI source[cite: 36]. The longer the blood passes through the intestines, the darker the stool color becomes due to the breakdown of hemoglobin and the release of iron[cite: 39].

13. Which of the following defines a "massive" upper GI hemorrhage?

Rationale: A massive UGI hemorrhage is defined as a loss of more than 1500 mL of blood or 25% of intravascular blood volume[cite: 68]. This life-threatening condition results in hypovolemic shock[cite: 67, 71].

14. True or False: Occult bleeding is a type of obvious bleeding, characterized by melena.

Rationale: False. Bleeding is categorized as obvious (hematemesis or melena) or occult[cite: 70]. Occult bleeding involves small amounts of blood in secretions or stools that are *not* apparent by appearance and are detectable only by a guaiac test[cite: 70].

15. A patient with a GI bleed has a urine output of 20 mL in the last hour. The patient weighs 70 kg. How should the nurse interpret this finding?

Rationale: A sign of hypovolemic shock is urine output less than 0.5 mL/kg/hr[cite: 99]. For a 70 kg patient, the minimum acceptable output is 35 mL/hr (70 * 0.5). An output of 20 mL/hr is below this threshold and indicates poor vital organ perfusion, which is why an indwelling catheter is inserted for accurate hourly assessment[cite: 130, 131].

16. What is the *primary* diagnostic and first-line *management* tool for UGI bleeding?

Rationale: Endoscopy is the primary tool for diagnosing the source of UGI bleeding [cite: 113] and is also the first-line management[cite: 138]. It is used for diagnosis, determining the need for surgery, and providing treatment (hemostasis)[cite: 138].

17. When is angiography used in the management of GI bleeding?

Rationale: Angiography is used when endoscopy cannot be done or when bleeding persists after endoscopic therapy[cite: 115]. It involves inserting a catheter into the femoral artery to find the bleeding site[cite: 116].

18. True or False: All critically ill patients in the ICU should be considered at risk for stress ulcers (SRES).

Rationale: True. Stress-related erosive syndrome (SRES) abnormalities can develop rapidly within hours of ICU admission[cite: 50]. The lecture states, "All critically ill patients should be considered at risk for stress ulcers and therefore GI hemorrhage"[cite: 52].

19. Which medication class, such as Omeprazole, is given via IV to decrease acid secretion during an acute bleed?

Rationale: In the emergency management phase, IV proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) like Omeprazole and pantoprazole are given to decrease acid secretion[cite: 132].

20. What is the goal of a Transjugular Intrahepatic Portosystemic Shunting (TIPS) procedure?

Rationale: TIPS is a radiologic procedure where a stent is placed to create a conduit between the hepatic and portal vein[cite: 144]. The purpose is to decrease portal pressure, which is the cause of esophagogastric varices. It's considered if endoscopic management of varices fails[cite: 144, 145].

21. A nurse is assessing a patient with a known GI bleed. Which finding is *most* indicative of a gastric perforation?

Rationale: One of the major complications to monitor for is gastric perforation. The key signs are a tense, rigid, board-like abdomen and the presence or absence of bowel sounds[cite: 213]. Agitation and restlessness are more classic signs of hypovolemic shock[cite: 197, 218].

22. True or False: A Sengstaken-Blakemore tube should only be placed *after* the patient's airway is secured.

Rationale: True. Placement of a Sengstaken-Blakemore or Minnesota tube is considered for patients with hemodynamic instability from known varices, but it "should be done only once the airway is secured"[cite: 142]. This procedure carries significant complication risks, including perforation[cite: 143].

23. A surgeon performs a "highly selective vagotomy." What is the physiological goal of this procedure?

Rationale: A highly selective vagotomy involves severing the section of the vagus nerve that innervates the parietal glands[cite: 149]. The goal is to decrease gastric acid secretions[cite: 149], which contribute to peptic ulcer disease.

24. Which is *not* considered an indication for surgical intervention for acute UGI bleeding?

Rationale: Indications for surgery include massive life-threatening bleeding, failed endoscopic therapy, and perforation[cite: 157, 158, 159]. A diagnosis of *H. pylori* is the *cause* of many ulcers [cite: 45] but is treated medically, not surgically.

25. A surgical procedure to remove the lower portion of the stomach, which contains the most acid-producing cells, is called...

Rationale: An antrectomy is the removal of the lower portion of the stomach (the antrum), which contains the most acid-producing cells[cite: 150].

26. True or False: A patient with acute GI bleeding should be encouraged to drink clear fluids to maintain hydration.

Rationale: False. In the ongoing monitoring and management phase, the patient should be kept NPO (nothing by mouth)[cite: 165]. Hydration and volume replacement are managed intravenously[cite: 125, 223].

27. Which physical exam finding would suggest a history of liver disease (and thus risk for varices)?

Rationale: Spider angiomas, jaundice, and peripheral edema are physical exam findings associated with liver disease [cite: 202], which causes portal hypertension and esophagogastric varices[cite: 62]. Cool, clammy skin and rapid respirations are signs of hypovolemic shock[cite: 201, 202]. A rigid abdomen suggests perforation[cite: 213].

28. What is the priority nursing intervention to prevent aspiration in a patient with massive vomiting of blood?

Rationale: The priority nursing intervention for the "Risk for Aspiration" diagnosis [cite: 170] is to maintain a patent airway, elevate the head of the bed, and have suction available at the bedside to prevent aspiration of emesis or blood[cite: 216].

29. True or False: A patient's stool may remain Hemoccult positive (positive for occult blood) for 1 to 2 weeks *after* a bleeding episode has stopped.

Rationale: True. It may take several days after bleeding stops for melenic stools to clear, and the stools may remain Hemoccult positive for 1 to 2 weeks[cite: 38].

30. Besides vital signs, what is one of the *best* measures of vital organ perfusion in a patient with acute GI bleeding?

Rationale: Urine output is one of the best measures of vital organ perfusion[cite: 130]. A decrease in urine output (specifically < 0.5 mL/kg/hr) is a key sign of hypovolemic shock[cite: 99].

31. A patient with a GI bleed suddenly becomes agitated, restless, and has a decreasing level of consciousness. The nurse recognizes these as primary signs of...

Rationale: Neurologic changes, including agitation, restlessness, and a decreasing level of consciousness, are key physical exam findings associated with hypovolemic shock[cite: 97, 197, 198, 218].

32. Which two factors are the *main* causes of peptic ulcer disease?

Rationale: The lecture states that two main causes contribute to the formation of peptic ulcers and UGI bleeding: the bacterial action of *H. pylori* (the leading cause) and the use of non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs)[cite: 44, 45, 46].

33. True or False: The severity of GI bleeding depends on whether the origin is venous, capillary, or arterial, with arterial bleeding being the most profuse.

Rationale: True. The severity of bleeding depends on whether the origin is venous, capillary, or arterial[cite: 29]. Bleeding from an arterial source is profuse and the blood is bright red[cite: 30].

34. Which of these is a common cause of *lower* GI hemorrhage?

Rationale: According to the etiology table, Diverticulosis is a cause of lower GI bleeding[cite: 41]. The other three options (varices, Mallory-Weiss, PUD) are all listed as causes of upper GI bleeding[cite: 41].

35. What is the goal of endoscopic hemostasis techniques like thermal therapy or injection therapy?

Rationale: Endoscopy is used for both diagnosis *and* treatment[cite: 138]. The specific goal of endoscopic hemostasis (which includes techniques like thermal therapy and injection therapy [cite: 140]) is to coagulate or thrombose the bleeding vessel[cite: 139].

36. A nurse is monitoring a patient post-endoscopy. Which potential complication requires immediate attention?

Rationale: Nursing interventions include monitoring the patient for potential complications of endoscopy or colonoscopy, which include perforation, sepsis, pulmonary aspiration, and induced bleeding[cite: 233]. These are severe complications requiring immediate intervention.

37. True or False: The Ligament of Treitz separates the esophagus from the stomach.

Rationale: False. The Ligament of Treitz is the anatomic division that separates the upper GI tract (esophagus, stomach, or duodenum) from the lower GI tract (jejunum, ileum, colon, or rectum)[cite: 22, 23, 25]. It specifically marks the division between the duodenum and the jejunum.

38. Which finding is *not* a characteristic sign of hypovolemic shock?

Rationale: Signs of hypovolemic shock include cool, clammy skin [cite: 95], increased heart rate (tachycardia) [cite: 96], decreased level of consciousness [cite: 97], and *decreased* urine output (less than 0.5 mL/kg/hr)[cite: 99]. Increased urine output would indicate adequate perfusion, not shock.

39. A patient is being discharged after a GI bleed. Which lifestyle modification is a key component of patient education?

Rationale: Patient education focuses on modifying precipitating factors[cite: 238]. This includes lifestyle changes such as stress management, diet modifications, alcohol cessation, and smoking cessation[cite: 241]. Aspirin and NSAIDs are precipitating factors and should be avoided[cite: 177].

40. The priority nursing diagnosis "Decreased Cardiac Output" in a patient with GI bleeding is related to...

Rationale: The nursing diagnosis "Decreased Cardiac Output" is listed as being "related to alterations in preload"[cite: 169]. The "absolute loss" of blood volume (deficient fluid volume) [cite: 168] reduces the amount of blood returning to the heart (preload), which in turn decreases cardiac output.
