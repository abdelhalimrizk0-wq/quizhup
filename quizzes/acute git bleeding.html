1. Which anatomical landmark is used to differentiate between Upper and Lower Gastrointestinal (GI) bleeding?

Rationale: The ligament of Treitz is the anatomic division used to differentiate between the two areas. Bleeding proximal to it is Upper GI, and distal is Lower GI.

2. Melena is characterized by foul-smelling, black, tarry stools. This clinical sign is indicative of an Upper GI source in what percentage of cases?

Rationale: Melena is indicative of upper gastrointestinal bleeding in 90% of cases. It occurs with slow bleeding from an upper GI source.

3. Which bacterial pathogen is cited as the leading cause of upper GI hemorrhage (50-70% of cases)?

Rationale: The bacterial action of Helicobacter pylori (H. pylori) is the leading cause of upper GI hemorrhage, accounting for 50-70% of cases.

4. Which of the following patients is at highest risk for developing Stress-Related Erosive Syndrome (Stress Ulcers)?

Rationale: Patients at high physiologic stress are at greatest risk. Specific risk factors mentioned include mechanical ventilation, extensive burns, severe trauma, shock, or acute neurologic disease.

5. What is the goal gastric fluid pH when monitoring a patient to prevent stress ulcers?

Rationale: Routine assessments should include gastric fluid pH monitoring with a goal of keeping the pH greater than 4.

6. Esophagogastric varices are most commonly a secondary result of which condition?

Rationale: Esophagogastric varices develop as a result of portal hypertension secondary to hepatic cirrhosis, a chronic disease of the liver.

7. A Mallory-Weiss tear is best described as:

Rationale: Mallory-Weiss tears are lacerations of the distal esophagus at the gastroesophageal junction, often associated with heavy alcohol use and forceful vomiting/retching.

8. Which of the following defines a "Massive" Upper GI hemorrhage?

Rationale: A massive UGI hemorrhage is defined as a loss of more than 1500 mL of blood or 25% of intravascular blood volume.

9. A nurse is reviewing the lab results of a patient with acute GI bleeding. Why might the initial Hematocrit (Hct) and Hemoglobin (Hb) be normal despite significant blood loss?

Rationale: Initial Hct may be normal because the loss of plasma and RBCs is initially equal. It may take 4 to 6 hours after fluid replacement or up to 72 hours for plasma redistribution to reflect the true loss.

10. What is the primary diagnostic tool for determining the source of Upper GI bleeding?

Rationale: Endoscopy is the primary tool for diagnosing the source of UGI bleeding. If performed within 12 hours, it has a 90% effectiveness rate in hemostasis.

11. A patient arrives in the ER with massive hematemesis. What is the PRIORITY nursing intervention?

Rationale: In any emergency, specifically with unresponsive or unstable patients, the priority is always Airway, Breathing, and Circulation (ABC). Intubation may be necessary for airway protection.

12. Which IV solution is recommended as the initial isotonic crystalloid for fluid resuscitation in GI bleeding?

Rationale: The text specifies administering isotonic crystalloid solution (e.g., lactated Ringer's solution) for fluid replacement therapy.

13. Why is an indwelling urinary catheter inserted in a patient with acute GI bleeding?

Rationale: Urine output is one of the best measures of vital organ perfusion. A catheter allows for accurate hourly assessment (target >0.5 mL/kg/hr).

14. Which pharmacological agent is administered specifically to decrease acid secretion?

Rationale: IV Proton Pump Inhibitors (PPI) therapy (Omeprazole & pantoprazole) are given to decrease acid secretion.

15. The Sengstaken-Blakemore tube is considered for which specific condition?

Rationale: It is used for hemodynamic instability/massive GI bleeds in cases of known **varices**. It is a temporary measure with significant complication risks.

16. What is the main purpose of a Transjugular Intrahepatic Portosystemic Shunt (TIPS)?

Rationale: TIPS is a radiologic procedure where a stent creates a conduit between the hepatic and portal vein to decrease portal pressure, often used when endoscopic management of varices fails.

17. Which surgical procedure involves "severing the section of the vagus nerve which innervates parietal glands"?

Rationale: A highly selective vagotomy involves severing the section of the vagus nerve that innervates parietal glands to decrease gastric acid secretions.

18. True or False: Upper GI hemorrhage is more common than Lower GI hemorrhage.

Rationale: The text explicitly states: "Upper GI hemorrhage is more common than lower gastrointestinal bleeding."

19. Hematochezia (bright red stools) most often signifies Lower GI bleeding, but it can occur in Upper GI bleeding if:

Rationale: Hematochezia could be due to a brisk upper GI bleeding with rapid transit time through the bowel in 10 to 15% of patients.

20. Which of the following is NOT listed as a cause of Lower GI bleeding in the etiology table?

Rationale: Mallory-Weiss tears are listed under Upper GI etiology. Diverticulosis, Hemorrhoids, and Angiodysplasia are listed under Lower GI.

21. NCLEX Style: A patient is admitted with suspected GI bleeding. They are pale, cool, and clammy. BP is 88/50, HR 120. Which assessment finding best indicates the patient is in hypovolemic shock?

Rationale: While hypotension and tachycardia are signs, the text specifically lists urine output <0.5 mL/kg/hr as a sign of hypovolemic shock and a measure of vital organ perfusion.

22. What is the recommended size for peripheral IVs when establishing access for a GI bleed patient?

Rationale: The management protocol requires establishing IV access with at least two large-bore peripheral IVs (18 gauge or larger).

23. In the context of endoscopic therapy, which of the following is NOT listed as a technique for hemostasis?

Rationale: Vagotomy is a SURGICAL therapy, not an endoscopic one. Thermal therapy, injection therapy, and intraarterial infusions are listed under endoscopic/hemostasis management.

24. What are the "Hallmarks" of GI hemorrhage?

Rationale: The text explicitly lists the Hallmarks of GI hemorrhage as Hematemesis, Hematochezia, and Melena.

25. When educating a patient on lifestyle modifications to prevent recurrence, which of the following is recommended?

Rationale: Patient education includes Stress management, Diet modifications, Alcohol cessation, and Smoking cessation.

26. NCLEX Style: The nurse notes the patient with a GI bleed has a rigid, board-like abdomen and absent bowel sounds. What complication should the nurse suspect immediately?

Rationale: Signs of Gastric perforation include a tense, rigid, board-like abdomen and the presence or absence of bowel sounds.

27. Which of the following is an indication for SURGICAL intervention in Acute Upper GI Bleeding?

Rationale: Indications for surgery include severe hemorrhage unresponsive to initial resuscitation, massive life-threatening bleeding, failed endoscopy, perforation, obstruction, or malignancy.

28. Antrectomy involves the removal of which portion of the stomach?

Rationale: Antrectomy is the removal of the lower portion of the stomach, which contains the most acid-producing cells.

29. Angiography is typically used when:

Rationale: Angiography is used when endoscopy cannot be done or when bleeding persists after endoscopic therapy.

30. Why is the patient kept NPO (Nothing by Mouth) initially?

Rationale: "Keep patient NPO" is listed under ongoing monitoring/management. This is standard practice to prevent aspiration of emesis and prepare for endoscopy/surgery.

31. What color is blood from an arterial source typically described as?

Rationale: Bleeding from an arterial source is profuse and bright red because it has not been in contact with gastric HCl acid secretion.

32. NCLEX Style: A patient has "coffee grounds" emesis. What does this appearance signify?

Rationale: Hematemesis appearing as brown "coffee grounds" emesis means that the blood has been in the stomach for some time.

33. Which nursing diagnosis focuses on "alterations in preload"?

Rationale: "Decreased Cardiac Output related to alterations in preload" is a specific nursing diagnosis listed in the text.

34. A segmental bowel resection with primary anastomosis is often necessary for:

Rationale: The text states: "A segmental bowel resection with a primary anastomosis is often necessary for definitive treatment of acute lower GI bleeding."

35. Vasopressin is administered via intraarterial infusion into the gastric artery to induce:

Rationale: Intraarterial infusion of vasopressin into the gastric artery is used to induce **vasoconstriction**, thereby helping to control bleeding.
