1. What is the definition of precordial shock?

Rationale: "Precordial shock is performed by passing an electric current across the chest wall and through the heart muscle, resulting in depolarization of the myocardial muscle fibers."

2. What is the goal of precordial shock?

Rationale: The goals listed are to "Resume control SAN," "Restore Pumping action," and "Restore electrical action."

3. What is the most effective therapy for terminating ventricular fibrillation?

Rationale: "Defibrillation is the most effective therapy for termination of ventricular fibrillation and ventricular tachydysrhythmias."

4. What is transthoracic impedance?

Rationale: "It's the resistance of the thorax to the flow of current and is measured to check whether the defibrillation electrodes are correctly attached..."

5. What is considered a normal range for transthoracic impedance?

Rationale: The document states "Normal (10-80) OHM, Poor technique (150) OHM."

6. Which of the following is NOT a type of precordial shock listed?

Rationale: The document lists Defibrillation (External and Internal, including AED and ICD) and Cardioversion (Chemical and Electrical). A precordial thump is a separate BLS maneuver not listed here as a type of precordial *shock*.

7. In the standard sterno-apical position, one paddle is placed to the right of the sternum below the clavicle, and the other is placed:

Rationale: "Standard sterno-apical: Right of the sternum below the clavicle, 5th intercostal space mid-axillary line."

8. In which position are paddles placed over the left pericardium and on the back (left or right infrascapular)?

Rationale: "Anterior-posterior position: Over left pericardium, back left or right infrascapular."

9. What is an Automated External Defibrillator (AED)?

Rationale: An AED "uses a computerized detection system, analyses cardiac rhythms, distinguishes between rhythms... and delivers a series of programmed electrical shocks."

10. The acronym "PAAD" for AED use stands for:

Rationale: "The technique for using AED (PAAD): P- Power on, A- Attach pads, A - Analyze, D - Defibrillate."

11. An AED should be applied only to patients who are:

Rationale: "AED should be applied only to Unresponsive, non-breathing and pulseless patients."

12. Manual external defibrillation is indicated for Ventricular Fibrillation (VF) and:

Rationale: "Indications of defibrillation: 1. Ventricular fibrillation (VF). 2. Pulseless ventricular tachycardia."

13. What is the key difference between a monophasic and a biphasic defibrillator?

Rationale: "Monophasic... provides a shock with current flow in one direction... Biphasic truncated technology... incorporates two-way current flow..."

14. Which type of defibrillator generally requires a higher energy level (e.g., 200-360 J) and has a lower success rate (60%)?

Rationale: "Monophasic... needs high joules to deliver shock (from 200 to 360 J), rate of success is (60%)."

15. Which type of defibrillator generally requires a lower energy level (e.g., 150-200 J) and has a higher success rate (90%)?

Rationale: "Biphasic... more effective and safe. Needs low joules to deliver shock (150-200 J), Rate of success (90%)."

16. What is the recommended external paddle electrode diameter for an infant?

Rationale: "Infant: 4 to 5 cms"

17. Which safety measure is critical before delivering a shock?

Rationale: All three are listed as safety measures to take "Before" the shock: "Remove metal object," "Remove transdermal patch," "Avoid excessive gel," and "Dry patient environment."

18. What is the MOST important safety action during the shock?

Rationale: While all are important, stating "Clear (ALL)" is the most critical safety step "to maintain safety to caregivers" and prevent accidental shock.

19. When defibrillating a patient with a permanent pacemaker, where should the paddles be placed?

Rationale: "in patient with a permanent pacemaker, paddle placement is in the opposite site... and at least 5cm from the pulse generator." Also stated as "Avoid placing the paddles over a pacemaker."

20. What is the recommended energy level for Ventricular Fibrillation (VF) using a monophasic defibrillator?

Rationale: Table 2 shows that for VF, the recommended monophasic energy level is 360J.

21. What is the recommended initial energy level for Atrial Fibrillation (AF) using a biphasic defibrillator?

Rationale: Table 2 shows that for AF, the recommended biphasic energy level is 120-200J. Table 1 also shows 100J (monophasic) or 120-200J (biphasic).

22. During the defibrillation procedure, why must the oxygen source be disconnected?

Rationale: "Disconnect oxygen source during actual defibrillation" in order "To decrease the risk of combustion in the presence of electrical current."

23. Why is 25lb/in of pressure applied to the paddles against the chest wall?

Rationale: "Apply 25lb/in pressure to each paddle... To decrease transthoracic resistance improves the flow of current across the axis of the heart."

24. If the third attempt at defibrillation (360J) is unsuccessful, what is the next step?

Rationale: "If third attempt is unsuccessful, initiate ACLS." This involves administering medications like Epinephrine or Amiodarone as per the algorithm.

25. Which of the following is a potential complication of defibrillation?

Rationale: The document lists all of these as potential complications: "Cerebral anoxia and brain death," "Respiratory complications," and "Burn."

26. What is direct internal defibrillation?

Rationale: "Direct internal defibrillation is achieved by delivering an electric current directly to the myocardium's surface."

27. Why are energy requirements (5-60J) much lower for internal defibrillation?

Rationale: "Direct internal defibrillation eliminates transthoracic resistance, so the recommended energy requirements are much lower than with external defibrillation."

28. An Implantable Cardioverter-Defibrillator (ICD) is a device implanted in the chest that:

Rationale: An ICD "is a device that is implanted in the chest to monitor for and if necessary correct episodes of rapid heartbeat."

29. Which of the following is an indication for an ICD?

Rationale: All three are listed as major indications for an ICD.

30. What is cardioversion?

Rationale: "Cardioversion is the therapy of choice for termination of hemodynamically unstable tachydysrhythmias."

31. Cardioversion is recommended for all the following rhythms EXCEPT:

Rationale: Ventricular Fibrillation (and pulseless VT) requires *defibrillation* (an unsynchronized shock), not cardioversion. Cardioversion is used for unstable tachycardias where a pulse is present.

32. What is the key difference between "chemical cardioversion" and "electrical cardioversion"?

Rationale: "Chemical cardioversion: refers to the use of antiarrhythmic medications... Electrical cardioversion... a synchronized electrical shock is delivered."

33. What is the purpose of the "synchronization" mode in electrical cardioversion?

Rationale: The rationale is "To sense the R wave so as to deliver the current outside the heart's vulnerable period" and "prevent the random delivery... which may potentiate ventricular fibrillation."

34. A potential complication of cardioverting atrial tachydysrhythmias is:

Rationale: All are listed as complications, but "Pulmonary and cerebral emboli" is a specific risk because "Atrial tachydysrhythmias predispose the patient to the development of microemboli that may be released during cardioversion."

35. Why is a patient made NPO (nothing by mouth) before an elective cardioversion?

Rationale: Patients are sedated for elective cardioversion. Being NPO is "To decrease the risk of aspiration."

36. For cardioversion, what must the nurse ensure about the ECG display before activating sync mode?

Rationale: "Select monitor lead display an R wave of sufficient amplitude... In most models, synchronization is achieved when the monitoring lead produces a tall positive R wave."

37. True or False: During cardioversion, the operator must press and *hold* the discharge buttons until the shock is delivered.

Rationale: True. Unlike defibrillation (which is instant), cardioversion has a slight delay as it waits for the R-wave. The operator must "Depress both buttons on the paddles simultaneously" and hold them.

38. What is the recommended FIRST attempt energy level for Ventricular Tachycardia *with a pulse*?

Rationale: Both tables (Table 1 and the AHA table) recommend an initial cardioversion of 50J for VT with a pulse.

39. What is the recommended FIRST attempt energy level for Atrial Flutter?

Rationale: Table 1 indicates 50J for the first attempt. The AHA table also suggests 50-100J for SVT/Flutter (or 25J, but 50J is also correct). *Correction: The second table lists 25J as a first attempt, while Table 1 lists 50J. Table 2 (Biphasic) lists 50-100J. 50J is a common and correct answer.*

40. What is the recommended pediatric defibrillation dose?

Rationale: The AHA table indicates the pediatric recommendation is to "Defibrillate with 2J/kg of body weight."
